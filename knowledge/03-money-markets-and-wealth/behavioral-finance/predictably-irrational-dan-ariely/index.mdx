## Overview

*Predictably Irrational: The Hidden Forces That Shape Our Decisions* (2008) is the book that brought behavioral economics into popular culture. Drawing on two decades of controlled laboratory experiments at MIT, Berkeley, and Duke, Dan Ariely demonstrates that human irrationality is not random noise — it is systematic, repeatable, and therefore predictable. His scope is vast, covering decisions from buying jeans to choosing a spouse, from saving for retirement to understanding why honest people cheat on taxes. Every chapter opens with a deceptively simple question — *Why does "FREE!" seem irresistible? Why don't we finish projects we started? Why does owning something change its perceived value?* — and answers it with elegant experiments that expose the hidden machinery of choice.

Ariely challenges the rational-choice assumptions that underpin standard economics. He does not argue that humans are *completely* irrational. He argues that we are predictably irrational — that our deviations from rationality follow identifiable patterns, that those patterns can be studied scientifically, and that once we understand them we can design better choice environments. Rather than attacking economics, he wants to improve it. The book was a *New York Times* bestseller, translated into more than thirty languages, and is credited with sparking the broader behavioral economics boom of the late 2000s. Its influence extends beyond academia into public policy (nudge theory), marketing, product design, and financial regulation. The book inspired the 2023 NBC television series *The Irrational*, starring Jesse L. Martin as a behavioral-science professor modeled on Ariely himself.
