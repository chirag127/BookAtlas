## Overview

*Misbehaving: The Making of Behavioral Economics* (2015) is Richard Thaler's arresting, frequently hilarious account of the four-decade struggle to bring an academic discipline back down to earth. Part memoir, part intellectual history, part attack on the Chicago School of economics, the book chronicles how Thaler and a small band of rebels documented the systematic ways in which real humans deviate from the fictional "Econ" of standard economic theory — and how the profession fought them every step of the way.

The book's central argument is simple but radical: the agents in an economy are not the perfectly rational, utility-maximizing *homo economicus* of textbook models. They are Humans — error-prone, present-biased, loss-averse, fairness-obsessed individuals whose decisions are shaped by cognitive shortcuts that work well in evolutionary environments but produce predictable errors in modern markets. Thaler spent his career cataloging these errors on a literal yellow-pad "List" that started in graduate school at Rochester in 1976 and eventually grew into an entire field. The endowment effect, mental accounting, the planner-doer model of self-control, the power of fairness norms, and the closed-end fund puzzle are all chapters in this story — each one an anomaly that mainstream economists tried to explain away, and each one ultimately vindicated.

What makes *Misbehaving* unique among behavioral economics books is its narrative form. Where Daniel Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow* is a systematic synthesis of decades of research, Thaler's book is a story about the people, the fights, the experiments, and the slow, grudging acceptance of ideas that were once dismissed as a sideshow. It is also a book about the real-world application of behavioral insights — from the Save More Tomorrow program that helped US employees save an additional $7.6 billion annually, to the UK's Behavioural Insights Team that increased tax revenue by £9 million in three weeks with a single redesigned letter. When economics meets psychology, the implications for individuals, managers, and policymakers are both profound and entertaining.
