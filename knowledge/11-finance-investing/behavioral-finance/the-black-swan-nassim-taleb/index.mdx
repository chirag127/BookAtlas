## Overview

*The Black Swan: The Impact of the Highly Improbable* is the second book
in Nassim Nicholas Taleb's five-volume Incerto series — a sweeping
investigation of uncertainty, probability, and the limits of human
knowledge. Published in April 2007, just one year before the 2008 global
financial crisis would make its thesis devastatingly real, the book argues
that the most consequential events in history are rare, unpredictable
outliers — Black Swans — that lie outside the realm of normal
expectation, carry extreme impact, and are only rationalized after the
fact.

Taleb's central claim is that we are systematically blind to Black Swans
because of three cognitive failures: we underestimate the role of rare
events in shaping history (the Black Swan blindness itself), we construct
compelling narratives that make past randomness appear deterministic (the
narrative fallacy), and we only see the survivors of risky processes
while the failures remain invisible (silent evidence). These failures are
not merely academic — they are embedded in the risk models used by banks,
governments, and corporations, creating a false sense of security that
makes catastrophic failures more likely, not less.

The book spent 36 weeks on the *New York Times* best-seller list (17 as
hardcover, 19 as paperback), was published in 32 languages, and has been
cited over 10,600 times as of 2020. *The Sunday Times* named it one of
the twelve most influential books since World War II. Nobel laureate
Daniel Kahneman wrote that it "changed my view of how the world works" and
acknowledged its influence in his own *Thinking, Fast and Slow*.
